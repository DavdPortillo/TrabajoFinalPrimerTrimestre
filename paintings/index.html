Paintings

The Creation of Adam
Michelangelo. 1508–1512

God is depicted as an elderly white-bearded man, wrapped in a swirling cloak while Adam, on the lower left, is completely naked. God's right arm is outstretched to impart the spark of life from his own finger into that of Adam, whose left arm is extended in a pose mirroring God's, a reminder that man is created in the image and likeness of God

Love and Pain
Edvard Munch. 1895

The painting shows a woman with long flame-red hair kissing a man on the neck, as the couple embrace.

Although others have seen in it "a man locked in a vampire's tortured embrace – her molten-red hair running along his soft bare skin," Munch himself always claimed it showed nothing more than "just a woman kissing a man on the neck". The painting was first called Vampire by Munch's friend, the critic Stanisław Przybyszewski. Przybyszewski saw the painting on exhibition and described it as "a man who has become submissive, and on his neck a biting vampire's face."

Fish Magic
Paul Klee. 1925

Fish Magic is seen as an intermingling of aquatic, celestial, and earthly entities. The painting is covered by a delicate surface of black paint, under which lies a dense layer of multicolored pigments. The colorful figures were then scratched and scrawled out by Klee on the dark background. A square of muslin was glued to the painting in the center, giving the painting the sense of a collage. The painting's dark palette and the muslin's fragility create a mysterious and inky atmosphere.

According to Ann Temkin, Fish Magic is a masterpiece in which the intellectual and imaginative forces of Klee's artistic gifts are reconciled, producing a "sense of magic".

Impression, Sunrise
Claude Monet. 1872

Monet shows us three rowing boats sailing through the port of La Havre, while in the background, between the morning mist and the smoke from the chimneys of the factories, port docks and boats, the sun rises.

Monet, trying to capture the atmosphere of nature in a fleeting moment, at the same moment, chooses not to clearly define the outline of the boats and everything is intermingled with the smoke from the chimneys and the mist. In relation to the colors, the cold ones predominate, highlighting the blue and gray tones, thus creating a tenuous mist that surrounds the entire canvas.

The School of Athens
Raffaello Sanzio. 1509-1511

Plato and Aristotle seem to be the central figures in the scene. Throughout the Middle Ages they were considered the main representatives of ancient philosophy, they are in the center of the composition, around the vanishing point. Plato is holding the Timaeus. Aristotle holds up a copy of his Nicomachean Ethics. Both discuss the search for Truth and make gestures that correspond to his interests in philosophy: Plato is pointing to the sky, symbolizing the dualistic rationalist idealism that is his thought; while Aristotle, the earth, referring to the teleological rational substantial realism of him.
